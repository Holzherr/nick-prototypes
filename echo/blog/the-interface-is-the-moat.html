The interface is the moat.

The most useful thing anyone has said about this wave of AI is also the most deflating: the model is not the product. We think Benedict Evans has the shape of it right — and it's the bet Echo is built on.

Evans' argument runs against the loudest story in the industry. The loud story says the frontier lab with the best model wins everything — that intelligence itself is the prize, and whoever holds the most of it holds the market. Evans' quieter claim is that the model is becoming infrastructure, like a browser engine or a database: necessary, invisible, and not where the money ends up.

Four moves make up the thesis, and we agree with all four.

Models are commoditising into infrastructure

Foundation models have no real network effects and no durable lock-in. The labs keep matching each other's capabilities within months — a lead measured in weeks, not years. When the underlying engine is adequate for most uses across every provider, raw model performance stops being a moat. It becomes a utility you buy by the token. Value moves up the stack, to the layer that turns that raw capability into something a person actually uses.

When the model is a commodity, the things that aren't commodities become the whole game.

Distribution beats being best

Because every provider's model is good enough, the winner is whoever owns the channel. Evans' evidence is hard to argue with: Google can jam Gemini into Android, Meta can push Llama across its apps, and a technically superior challenger can simply stall against that distribution. This is the direct counter to the assumption that any one lab's lead is defensible. In his "How will OpenAI compete?" piece he makes it bluntly: no unique technology, weak engagement, no network effect — while the incumbents have matched the tech and already own the front door.

Chat is a bad interface — and the labs can't build the apps

This is the piece most people skip, and it's the one we care about most. A blank chat box is a terrible general-purpose interface. It asks the user to know what to ask, to phrase it well, and to do the work of assembling the answer into something useful. Real general-purpose use needs purpose-built experiences — and there is a near-infinite surface area of them. The labs structurally cannot build, or auto-generate, all of those apps. So innovation and value migrate up the stack, to the people who do build them.

As big as the internet — and only as big

The framing wrapper is deliberately contrarian. Evans is bullish: this is a genuine platform shift on the scale of the internet or the smartphone. But he pushes back on the everything-changes-overnight narrative. We're at something like 1997 — transformative, but the value accrues through normal competitive dynamics: distribution, use cases, experiences. Not one lab winning the world.

So here's the bet

If the model is a commodity, then the durable advantages are the things sitting above it. That's not a hedge for us — it's the entire design of Echo.

  • We don't compete on the model. We treat it as a swappable engine. When a better or cheaper one ships, we change a line of config. The intelligence is rented; what we build on top is owned.
  • Memory is the asset. The thing that compounds isn't the model — it's everything Echo knows about your work and your life: every person, every promise, every open thread. That context is yours, it travels with you, and it gets more valuable every day. A commoditised model can't take it away.
  • Distribution means meeting you where you already are. We're not asking you to open another app. Echo lives on the channels you already use — Telegram, WhatsApp, Slack, email, voice — as one assistant with one memory. Ambient, not another tab.
  • The interface is doing the work, not chatting. Echo doesn't hand you a blank prompt and wish you luck. It composes purpose-built surfaces — the morning brief, the approval queue, the board of what's slipping — and does the work before you ask. The experience is the product.
  • Ownership is the moat you can't be evicted from. Your VM, your key, your database, an open core. If the model layer is a utility, the relationship and the memory are what's defensible — and those should belong to you, not to a lab renting you access to your own life.

Evans' two takeaways are the spine of all of it: distribution is the moat once models commoditise, and value accrues to the layer above the model, not the model itself. The interesting work — and the durable value — is in the interface, the memory, and the relationship. That's the layer we're building. That's where we intend to live.

The labs will keep making the engines better and cheaper. Good — we'll take them. We're not trying to win the race they're running. We're building the thing that's still standing when that race is over: the assistant that knows you, comes to you, and has already done the work by the time you wake up.
