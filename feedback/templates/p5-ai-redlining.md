## What this is

AI-suggested-changes layered on top of standard track-changes. The AI looks at a contract, proposes redlines and explanatory comments, and the user accepts / rejects / refines each one. Same workflow you'd use in Word, but with an AI collaborator generating the first-pass edits.

There are two parallel threads:

- **P5 — Nick's fork**: https://gitlaw-co.github.io/gitlaw-redline-nick/ — the one to focus on.
- **P6 — Dario's main**: https://gitlaw-co.github.io/gitlaw-redline/ — upstream Nick's fork was cut from. Open it for comparison if you want.

Write feedback in this Doc regardless of which one you spent time in (note which when relevant).

## What to do

1. Open the prototype, click through the document with the AI suggestions visible.
2. Try **accepting** a suggestion, **rejecting** one, and **refining** one if there's a way to do so.
3. Toggle between the redline view and the track-changes view.
4. If you can imagine a counterparty seeing your accepted edits, do they look like *your* edits (good) or like AI edits (less good)?

---

## What we want feedback on (UX-focused)

1. **Trust gradient** — for which kinds of changes would you accept the AI's suggestion immediately vs require manual review? Where does the line sit for you?
2. **Suggestion explanations** — do the rationales help, or are they noise? When would you want more / less detail?
3. **Counterparty-facing output** — after accepting, do the resulting track-changes look natural? Anything that gives away the AI origin in a way that'd embarrass you in front of opposing counsel?
4. **Comments alongside redlines** — useful to have the AI's reasoning preserved as a comment? Or do they clutter the doc?
5. **What's missing** — given you do this all day in Word, what's the obvious gap in this prototype?
6. **Mistake recovery** — when the AI gets a suggestion wrong (and it will), is the rejection UX good enough that you'd keep using it, or does one bad suggestion poison the flow?
